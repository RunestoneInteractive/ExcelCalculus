Example5.2.19

Given the permutation \begin{equation*}\pi = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\end{equation*} on \(S = \{ 1,2,3 \}\), it is easy to see that the permutation defined by \begin{equation*}\pi^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\end{equation*} is the inverse of \(\pi\). In fact, any bijective mapping possesses an inverse, as we will see in the next theorem.
